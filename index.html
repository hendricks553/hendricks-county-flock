Community Notice · Unofficial Hendricks Co., Indiana

License plate readers are tracking every car in this county.

108 cameras. Six police agencies. Every plate read searchable through Flock's nationwide network — and zero state rules on how long your location data is kept or who sees it. Here's what's documented, and how to weigh in.

The short version 30 seconds

These cameras photograph every passing car — not just suspects — and log plate, location, and time in a searchable database. Indiana sets no limits on retention, sharing, or oversight, so every safeguard is voluntary and can change without notice. The ask is simple: end the contracts, or bind them with real rules.

What to ask for in order of preference

  1. Don't renew the contract. Bloomington did exactly this in March. The county functioned before these cameras; it will function after.
  2. Failing that: binding, public rules. A council-adopted use policy (not a vendor webpage), independent audits of search logs, and a public answer on whether local data feeds Flock's nationwide search network.
  3. At minimum: no quiet renewals. Cost, term, camera count, and retention discussed in the open — not passed as a line item.

Pick one thing any one helps

  1. 01
    Show up. A full room changes the conversation. You don't have to say a word.
  2. 02
    Speak (2–3 min). The three asks above are a ready outline. Sign-up is usually a sheet at the door.
  3. 03
    File a records request. Any person can request a town's Flock contract, payments, and use policy — residency not required (IC 5-14-3). Request forms: Avon · Brownsburg · Danville · Plainfield
    What to type
    I'd like copies of: (1) any contracts or agreements with Flock Safety, including the original and any renewals or amendments; (2) any invoices or payment records under those agreements; and (3) any policy, ordinance, or written guidance the town or police department has adopted governing use of its license plate reader cameras. Electronic copies by email are preferred. If any part of this request would involve a fee, please provide an estimated cost before proceeding. This request is for personal, noncommercial civic purposes.

    Paste as-is into any of the four forms. The last sentence matters — at least one local town has denied a request by presuming commercial intent, and stating your purpose up front removes that basis.

  4. 04
    Map a camera. See one that's not on deflock.me? Drop a pin. That's where the 108 count comes from.
  5. 05
    Sign the petition & email your state legislators. Indiana's zero-rules status is the root problem, and only the General Assembly can fix it — the state's only ALPR bill, in 2015, died in committee without a vote. Sign the Eyes Off Indiana petition, then find your legislators at iga.in.gov — they track how many constituents from each community reach out.
  6. 06
    Share this page. That's the whole ask.

What Flock publishes about local use

Flock hosts "transparency portals" for local agencies. Avon links its portal from the town website — credit where due; the others were surfaced by volunteers rather than linked by the departments. Three are reviewed here (numbers as of Jul 21, 2026):

Plainfield is the only local portal that publishes its sharing lists — and they answer the question the others leave open: every other Hendricks County agency (the Sheriff's Office, Avon, Brownsburg, Danville, Pittsboro, and Lizton) appears on both sides of those lists. All six local deployments participate in Flock's sharing network. Only Plainfield says so publicly.

How far the sharing reaches

Plainfield's data is searchable by agencies across 30+ states — including five federal entities (among them the US Postal Inspection Service and Wright-Patterson Air Force Base), state corrections and revenue departments as far away as Alabama, and prosecutors' offices from New Jersey to California. The lists even carry stale entries like "(Dead/Old) Plymouth IN PD" — access hygiene worth its own question.

The portals state
  • Plates & vehicles only — no facial recognition
  • Law-enforcement use only; never sold
  • Immigration enforcement prohibited
  • 30-day retention; access logs kept indefinitely
The open questions
  • Between 1 in 14 and 1 in 4½ passing cars (7–22%) is flagged as a "hit." How many were false positives — and how many led to a stop?
  • The Sheriff's and Avon's portals omit their sharing lists. Will they publish them, as Plainfield does?
  • Who reviews the access logs — has anyone, ever?
  • Were any of these policies adopted by a council or board, or do they exist only on Flock's site?

"Never sold" is likely true — and beside the point. No money changes hands for any of the sharing above: one query through the network can reach cameras across the country under the querying agency's rules, not ours.

An hour away, this already worked

Bloomington ended its Flock contract in March 2026.

After months of public pressure, the city let its ~$50,000 contract expire on March 5 and declined to renew. Even before that, it had barred out-of-state agencies from querying its database; afterward, it cut all outside data sharing.

The mayor's own words are the tone worth borrowing: if a tool like this is used at all, it must be under narrow parameters, strong accountability, and clear public safeguards.

What Avon's contract shows
  • It renews itself. After the initial 60-month term (signed 2023, running into ~2028), the agreement auto-renews in 24-month increments unless either party gives notice 30 days before the term ends. No action is a renewal decision.
  • Network sharing is opt-in. Flock's own proposal describes national and statewide sharing as something agencies "opt into" — so restricting sharing is a checkbox a council can direct, not a reason to cancel or keep anything.
  • Searches go beyond plates. The system's filters include vehicle make, decals, bumper stickers, and racks — a car can be found without its plate ever being read.
  • The full terms live on Flock's website. The signed order form incorporates a Terms of Service by URL; the complete legal agreement isn't in the council packet.
  • The money: $15,000/year for 6 cameras ($75,000 over five years), paid from Avon's economic-development (EDIT) fund. The 2023 deal locked $2,500/camera as Flock raised list price to $3,000.
What Brownsburg's records show
  • No use policy exists. A records request asking for any policy or written guidance governing the cameras was fulfilled with contracts and invoices only — the cameras operate under Flock's terms of service and nothing locally adopted.
  • ~$183,700 paid to Flock since 2021. 12 cameras in 2021 ($30k/yr), two added in 2022 on an invoice marked "(Walmart)," then a 2023 expansion to 16 cameras at $40,000/yr — a $201,300 five-year agreement with 24-month auto-renewals.
  • The order form was signed by the Chief of Police, with "IN - Brownsburg PD" as the customer — whether the council voted on the agreement is an open question.
  • Network sharing is in the base package: the signed FlockOS "Essentials" tier includes statewide and nationwide plate lookup, direct sharing with surrounding jurisdictions, and full access to privately owned Flock cameras shared with the PD.
